What this is

A taste-discovery layer for your Plex library.

Taste Graph helps you move through your Plex library by feel instead of by shelf label. It uses enrichment, scores, and relationship data to show which titles belong in the same neighborhood, which ones bridge different moods, and which picks best match the energy you want right now.

What is this?

Taste Graph turns a Plex library into an interactive map of taste. Every title becomes a node. Titles with similar tone, themes, pacing, weirdness, emotional weight, and other taste signals connect to one another. Instead of browsing by genre alone, you can explore by vibe.

The result is less like a catalog and more like a neighborhood map. One title pulls you toward nearby titles that feel related for reasons you can actually see and ask about.

Why not genre?

Genre is useful, but it is broad. Sci-fi can mean 2001, Blade Runner, Star Wars, or something wildly off-center. Horror can mean body horror, supernatural dread, slasher energy, surreal nightmare, or comedy-horror.

Taste Graph tries to map what titles feel like, not just what shelf they sit on.

How the graph works

  • Titles are imported from Plex.
  • Each title is enriched with tone, theme, style, mood, and signal scores.
  • Titles receive Johnny-core, Weirdness, and Emotional weight scores.
  • Meaningful shared traits generate connections.
  • Clusters and bridges make the map readable, so you can move from one title to the next by feel instead of guessing.

What the scores mean

Johnny-coreHow strongly a title matches your personal taste gravity.
WeirdnessHow strange, surreal, or off-center it feels.
Emotional weightHow intense, heavy, stressful, or psychologically loaded it is.

These are taste signals, not objective ratings.

Mapped vs edge-light titles

Mapped titles have enough strong similarity to earn a clear place on the main graph. Some titles stay more lightly connected: they still exist in the library, still carry enrichment, and still contribute to the broader taste picture, but they do not dominate the main map.

That does not mean those titles are mistakes. It usually means they are unusual, transitional, or simply less anchored to one clear neighborhood than the strongest mapped titles.

The AI layer

Ask Taste Graph uses the graph and enrichment data to answer questions like what is closest to something, what is weirder, why two titles connect, or what should you watch next.

When it can, the app uses local graph data first for fast, grounded similarity answers, then leans on AI for richer explanation.

What this is not

Not a Plex server monitorNo bandwidth, CPU, RAM, transcoding, or NAS health views here.
Not a replacement for PlexIt sits on top of your library as a taste-discovery layer.
Not a critic score systemIt is not trying to say what is objectively good.
Not genre metadataIt is about vibe, tone, and emotional relationship.
